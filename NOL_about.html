About

This started with a question I couldn't answer.

Notes on Listening is a publication about what music actually does to us — the emotions, the obsessions, the communities it creates, and the science behind it all. No expertise required. Just questions, research, and a lot of feelings.

Articles · Deep Cuts · Interviews

I've always felt music differently.

I don't remember a time when music was just background noise. It was never just a song. It was a place, a feeling, a version of myself I could only access through sound. I listened to the same albums on repeat for months. I built playlists for every emotion I didn't know how to name. I felt understood by artists I had never met — and for a long time, I thought everyone did.

I thought the obsession was universal. That the intensity, the attachment, the way a single chord progression could rearrange something inside me — I assumed everyone felt that. I never thought to question it.

Until I realized they didn't.

I was talking to someone close to me about music. She didn't feel it the same way. The obsession, the attachment, the intensity. She looked at me like I was speaking another language.

And I realized — not everyone feels it this way. Not everyone lies awake because of a harmony. Not everyone mourns when a tour ends. Not everyone has spent three months with the same album and found something new in it every single time.

That conversation started everything. Because if not everyone felt this way — then why did I? What was happening in my brain, in my body, when music did what it did to me? Was it something psychological? Neurological? Was it about the music, or about me?

So I started looking for answers.

I went looking for something that explored all of this. The science behind musical frisson. The psychology of parasocial attachment to artists. Why we share songs with people we love. Why certain voices move us in ways we can't explain. Why music is the last thing Alzheimer's patients forget.

I found research papers. I found fan forums. I found music criticism and neuroscience articles and a handful of podcasts. But I couldn't find something that brought it all together — something personal and researched, emotional and curious, written from the inside rather than the outside.

I'm not a psychologist. Not a researcher. Just someone who feels music deeply — and needed to know why. So I built Notes on Listening.

"Not everyone feels music this way. That's exactly why this exists."

What is Notes on Listening

Where the questions are welcome. Where the feelings make sense.

Notes on Listening is an independent publication that explores what music actually does to us — emotionally, neurologically, socially. It's published quarterly, with each issue built around a theme and structured like a magazine: a featured essay, a Deep Cut, a First Listen, a curated playlist, and an open question for the community.

The writing is personal and researched. It starts from lived experience — from the obsessions, the attachments, the moments where a song changed something — and then asks why. It looks at the science without losing the feeling. It takes fandom seriously without pathologising it.

No expertise required. Just curiosity, and a willingness to sit with the questions music raises about who we are.
