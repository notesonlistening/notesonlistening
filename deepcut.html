Deep Cut · Your Brain on Music Issue 00

Why some people get chills from music — and others don't

There's a name for it. And it has nothing to do with how much you love music.

Some people cry at concerts. Others stand there, perfectly fine, wondering what they're missing.

Neither is doing it wrong.

Research points to one personality trait that predicts who gets chills from music and who doesn't: Openness to Experience. People high in Openness engage with music cognitively — they follow the structure, they anticipate where it's going. And that active listening is exactly what makes the unexpected land harder.

When the song does something they didn't see coming — a key change, a silence held too long, a note that shouldn't work but does — the brain registers it as significant. The surprise is bigger. The emotional response follows. Their brains, scans show, have stronger connections between the auditory cortex and the areas that process emotion.

50%

of people never experience frisson — the physical chills that music causes. Goosebumps. A shiver down the spine. Something moving through the chest. It's a real neurological event. And roughly half the people you know have never felt it.

It's called frisson. And it isn't just emotional — it's cognitive. The more you listen with your brain, the more your skin reacts.

So if you've ever tried to describe that physical shiver to someone and watched them look at you blankly — they weren't being dismissive. They genuinely don't feel it. Their brain isn't wired for it.

Which means that for about half the people you know, music has never once touched their skin.

And which also means this: technically, you can train yourself to feel it more. Engage more. Predict more. Let yourself be wrong about where the song is going. The surprise is the whole point.
