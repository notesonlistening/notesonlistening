Feature · Moved

Dancing at
Midnight

I wasn't supposed to listen to it there. I was at my sister's. The album had just dropped and I couldn't wait — not even long enough to get home, not even long enough to have both earbuds in.

Somewhere in the middle of all that dancing, I started wondering — what is actually happening right now? Why is music making me this happy? Why this specific music? What I found is that anticipating music activates the same reward circuit in the brain as basic needs — and, as it turns out, cocaine and amphetamines.

Deep Cut · Your Brain on Music

There's a name for the chills music gives you.

You know that feeling — a song hits a certain chord, a voice does something unexpected, and suddenly your skin reacts. Goosebumps. A shiver. Something moving through your chest. It's called frisson. And roughly 50% of people never experience it at all.

First Listen

Notes in real time

No conclusions. No editing. Just what happened when I pressed play for the first time — written as it happened.

0:00

Okay. Here we go. Headphones on. Volume up. I've been waiting for this for three months.

Track 1

The opening is quieter than I expected. Almost like he's holding back. Already I can feel it — that specific thing when you recognise a voice and your whole body relaxes.

Track 3

I'm pacing. I don't realise I'm doing it until I notice I'm in the kitchen. How did I get here.

Track 5

This one. This one is it. I don't know what it is yet but it's the one I'll listen to 200 times. Already I know.

Track 7

A specific chord just happened and I stopped walking. Just stood there. That's the thing — music can make you freeze mid-step.

End

I'm going to listen again from the beginning. I was already thinking that before the last song finished. That thought alone — the anticipation of pressing play again — is making me happy.

Recommends

Beyond the music

One cultural object that resonates with the theme of this issue. A film, a book, a show — whatever is living alongside the music right now.

Book

This Is Your Brain on Music

Daniel J. Levitin

The book that started everything for me. A neuroscientist who was a record producer first — so he writes about music the way someone who has loved it writes. If you want to understand why music does what it does to you, this is the beginning.

Film

20 Feet from Stardom

Dir. Morgan Neville, 2013

A documentary about backup singers — people whose voices you've heard thousands of times without knowing their names. It's about invisibility, about what it costs to be close to something great without being the centre of it. It stayed with me for days.
