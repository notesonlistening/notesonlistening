Notes on Listening
May 2025

On beginnings, and the songs that mark them.

There is a specific kind of clarity that comes when you hear a song for the first time and realize — somehow, immediately — that it will matter to you. Not in the polite way that good music matters. In the other way. The way that means you'll still remember exactly where you were standing when you heard it, ten years from now.

"I didn't set out to build a publication. I set out to find answers to questions that felt too personal to ask out loud."

Notes on Listening exists because I couldn't find it anywhere else. A place that took seriously the emotional life we build around music — not just the songs themselves, but what they do to us. The way an artist can feel like a close friend. The way a chord progression can unlock a memory you didn't know you still had. The way a new album release can feel, inexplicably, like an event.

This first issue is called The First Time — because beginnings deserve to be examined. The first song that broke you open. The first time you stayed up all night to hear something new. The first time you realized not everyone feels music the way you do.

Welcome. Pull up a chair.

Feature

Music · Brain · Emotion

What happens in your brain
when you hear that song.

The neuroscience of anticipation, dopamine, and why a new album release feels like falling in love — even when you're alone in your apartment at midnight.

L
Notes on Listening  ·  8 min read  ·  Issue 00

It was a Friday in March. I was at my sister's apartment when the album dropped, and I did what I always do — I pressed play immediately, in a room that wasn't mine, with noise in the background, because I couldn't wait. The first listen was incomplete. Interrupted. And still, by the second song, I knew it was going to change something in me.

When I finally got home, I put my headphones on and turned the volume all the way up. And then I danced, alone, for hours. I cooked to it. I cleaned to it. I texted three people who didn't care about music the way I do, trying to explain something I couldn't quite put into words.

What I didn't know then — what I've spent months researching since — is that what I was experiencing was neurologically legible. There was a name for it. There was chemistry behind it. The joy wasn't random. It was dopamine, doing exactly what dopamine is supposed to do.

Here's what the science says: when your brain encounters music it loves, it releases dopamine — the same neurotransmitter associated with food, sex, and other pleasures. But here's what's fascinating. Researchers at McGill University found that dopamine doesn't just spike when the rewarding moment arrives. It spikes before — in anticipation of what's coming. The brain, essentially, is already celebrating before the thing has happened.

Which means the three weeks of waiting for an album isn't just endurance. It's neurological preparation. Your brain is warming up for something it already knows will matter.

Why the wait feels better
than the thing itself.

There is a paradox at the heart of anticipation: the moment we've been waiting for is almost never as good as the waiting itself. Not because the thing disappoints — but because the brain has already lived it, over and over, in the weeks before.

2×

Dopamine release during anticipation vs. the moment of reward itself, in several music studies

This is the dopamine anticipation effect: the reward circuitry activates earlier and more intensely in the buildup phase. By the time the album actually arrives, part of your brain has already processed the peak. What remains is something quieter — the pleasure of confirmation.

It's why the first listen is often less overwhelming than the 47th. The song needs time to become yours.

Track 3. Okay. This one is going to be the one. I don't know what it's about yet but something in the production — there's a chord change around the 1:40 mark that does something to my chest.
